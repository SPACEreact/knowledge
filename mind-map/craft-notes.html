üé≠ Character Creation

The Character Grid

Characters pursue either a Need (soul-growth, truth, internal shift) or a Want (external goal, desire, image). The World/Power can support or oppose each.

Character Type Pursues Power Supports Power Opposes
Conscious Need Harmonious story of growth Tragic hero, painful but transformative
Conscious Want Success arc, fulfillment Moral dilemma, sacrifice needed
Unconscious Need Accidental healing, fate helps Resistance until awakening
Unconscious Want Hollow success, irony Self-destruction, powerful conflict

Core Layers of a Character

  • Outer Mask ‚Äî Who they pretend to be (public persona)
  • Private Self ‚Äî Who they think they are (with trusted people)
  • Core Wound/Truth ‚Äî Who they are underneath (revealed in crisis)

Social Reaction Shifts

Characters shift depending on who's watching:

  • With someone they admire ‚Äî Performative, seeking validation
  • With someone they fear ‚Äî Obedient, deceptive, or defensive
  • With someone they hate ‚Äî Aggressive, mocking, combative
  • With someone they love ‚Äî Vulnerable or scared to be vulnerable
  • With someone they pity ‚Äî Superior, nurturing, or manipulative
"We are who we think they think we are."

Enneagram Types (Shorthand)

  • Type 9 ‚Äî Feels they have potential but chooses unhealthy way to come on top. Obsessive about achieving it. If they come on top and drop negative habits ‚Üí good leader.
  • Type 8 ‚Äî Less ego, more control. Wants to dominate to protect. Easy to write, straightforward. Won't show emotions. Learned they need to be tough.
  • Type 7 ‚Äî Avoids the darkness which stops them from becoming true self. Will do multiple things just to avoid the pain.

Character Introduction Rules

  • What is the first thing the character says? (Sherlock's first dialogue is deduction ‚Äî that's first impression)
  • Show what is the problem of the character
  • Make them likeable
  • Widen audience perception about themselves (Walter White says it's for family, but it was ego)
  • Break the stereotype (old lady we imagine to be "go with the flow" ‚Äî what if they're mean?)
  • Make them do something that contradicts expected behavior
Character should be split between 3 times:
Past ‚Äî A past that affects their present
Present ‚Äî Immediate problem (one personal conflict + one internal conflict)
üìê Story Structure

Dan Harmon's Story Circle

1. You ‚Äî Character in their zone of comfort
2. Need ‚Äî Wants something
3. Go! ‚Äî Enters an unfamiliar situation
4. Struggle ‚Äî Has to adapt
5. Find ‚Äî Gets what they want (or a version)
6. Suffer ‚Äî Has to make a sacrifice
7. Return ‚Äî Returns to familiar situation
8. Change ‚Äî Having changed fundamentally

Conflict Rules

"The real unity of opposites is one in which compromise is impossible."

The Unity of Opposites: Great conflict comes from characters with opposing needs, values, or goals, who are locked together in a situation where neither can back down or walk away.

  • Don't make inciting incident same for everyone ‚Äî protagonist should have higher stakes
  • It's never too soon to push character out of comfort zone (inciting incident)
  • Don't make conflict too easily resolved
  • A chain reaction of conflict should happen
For audience connection: Highlight the ordinary, make it extraordinary. People should relate but think "I'm not even in that bad a situation."
‚úçÔ∏è Scene Writing

The Three Questions

1. Who wants what from whom?
2. What happens if they don't get it?
3. Why now?

Scene Writing Process

  1. Write it simply: what does my character want, how did the scene change them?
  2. Rewrite with deeper context: how did the character react, what's the new correlation between characters now?
"Dialogue becomes powerful when characters have hidden agendas."
Add subtext in scene to represent something but don't demand any conclusion ‚Äî just leave it there.

What to Include

  • Camera movement
  • Aspect ratio
  • Character movement
  • Framing & composition
  • Angle
  • Transition between frames
  • Depth of field (cross out out-of-focus elements)

Visual Storytelling Rules

  • Movement of character should give sense of what they would do ‚Äî audience should anticipate
  • Show weather, time of day
  • Cut on action
  • Switch between full to medium shot in a single scene
üí° Lighting Notes

Types of Light Sources

  • Natural Light ‚Äî Sunlight, moonlight. Changes throughout the day.
  • Practical Light ‚Äî Existing lights in a scene (lamps, candles, screens).
  • Artificial Light ‚Äî Studio lights, LEDs, flash, strobes.

Three-Point Lighting

  • Key Light ‚Äî Strongest, main source, shapes the subject
  • Fill Light ‚Äî Softer, reduces shadows from key light
  • Backlight ‚Äî Behind subject, separates from background
  • Kicker Light ‚Äî Side/back, adds highlight on edge
  • Motivated Lighting ‚Äî Artificial lights mimicking natural sources

Lighting Styles

Style Description
Rembrandt Small triangle of light on one cheek, dramatic yet natural
Loop Slight shadow under nose, common for portraits
Split One half lit, other dark ‚Äî high contrast
Butterfly Shadow under nose, used for beauty shots
Clamshell Two soft lights above/below, smooth look
Silhouette Light from behind, subject appears dark
Low-Key High contrast, dramatic shadows (film noir)
High-Key Bright, low contrast (commercials, comedies)

Light Softness Rules

  • Larger light source = Softer shadows
  • Smaller light source = Harsher shadows
  • Closer light = Softer (wraps around subject)
  • Farther light = Harsher (more directional)
Shoot from the Dark Side ‚Äî Position camera on shadowed side of face for depth and drama.

Broad Lighting ‚Äî Bright side faces camera (makes face look wider)
Short Lighting ‚Äî Shadowed side faces camera (adds contrast and depth)
üìù Screenplay Conventions

Formatting Rules

  • Keep it visual and in present tense
  • Write V.O. for voice over
  • Add CUT TO or BACK TO for scene change
  • MONTAGE START and MONTAGE END
  • Dialogues in the centre
