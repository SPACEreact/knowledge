Where design starts moving ‚Äî and where most designers start lying

Motion graphics is design in time. Everything that applies to static design applies here ‚Äî hierarchy, contrast, balance ‚Äî but now you're also controlling when things happen and how they transform. The biggest trap: making things move because you can, not because the motion communicates something.

A

Fundamentals

The non-negotiable principles. These apply to everything from title sequences to 3-second Instagram animations.

Motion Design Principles

Motion design is not animation with a brand. It's the discipline of using movement to communicate information, create emotion, and guide attention. The core principles: every element should move with purpose, every transition should carry meaning, and every pause should give the viewer time to process. The hierarchy of motion design: clarity first, beauty second, "cool" never. If the viewer doesn't understand what they're looking at within the first second, the design has failed ‚Äî no matter how smooth the animation.

Animation Principles (12 Principles of Animation)

Created at Disney in the 1930s, these 12 principles remain the foundation of all motion ‚Äî 2D, 3D, motion graphics, and even AI:

1. Squash & Stretch ‚Äî Objects deform to show weight and flexibility. A bouncing ball squashes on impact.
2. Anticipation ‚Äî Before a big action, a small preparatory movement. A character crouches before jumping.
3. Staging ‚Äî Composition and positioning that make the action clear to the audience.
4. Straight Ahead vs Pose to Pose ‚Äî Two workflow approaches: drawing frame-by-frame vs key poses first.
5. Follow Through & Overlapping ‚Äî Different parts of a body stop at different times. Hair continues after the head stops.
6. Slow In & Slow Out ‚Äî Movement accelerates from rest and decelerates to stop. Nothing in nature moves at constant speed.
7. Arcs ‚Äî Natural movement follows curved paths, not straight lines.
8. Secondary Action ‚Äî Supporting movements that enrich the main action (a character walking while adjusting their hat).
9. Timing ‚Äî The number of frames between key poses determines perceived speed and weight.
10. Exaggeration ‚Äî Push beyond realism for clarity and impact.
11. Solid Drawing ‚Äî Understanding 3D form, weight, and balance in a 2D medium.
12. Appeal ‚Äî Characters and movements should be interesting to watch. Even a villain should be visually compelling.

Timing and Spacing

Timing is how many frames an action takes (fast or slow). Spacing is the distance between positions in each frame (even or accelerating). Together they control the perceived weight, speed, and emotion of every movement. Fast timing + even spacing = mechanical (robotic). Slow timing + eased spacing = gentle, heavy, or contemplative. Quick timing + tight spacing at start, wide at end = explosion. The same object animated with different timing and spacing can feel like a feather or a bowling ball.

Easing Curves

Easing defines how an object accelerates and decelerates between keyframes. Linear motion (no easing) feels robotic and lifeless ‚Äî nothing moves at constant speed. Ease In ‚Äî starts slow, accelerates (falling object). Ease Out ‚Äî starts fast, decelerates (landing object). Ease In-Out ‚Äî slow start, fast middle, slow end (most natural motion). Custom curves ‚Äî asymmetric easing for character: a quick snap followed by a gentle settle creates personality. Easing is the difference between motion that feels "computer-generated" and motion that feels alive. Master the graph editor.

Anticipation and Follow Through

Anticipation: Before any major action, there's a small opposite movement. A ball compresses before bouncing. A character pulls their arm back before throwing. A UI element briefly shrinks before expanding. Anticipation tells the viewer "something is about to happen" ‚Äî it's nature's way of preparing the eye.
Follow Through: After the main action stops, secondary elements continue briefly. Hair settles after a head stops turning. A menu overshoots its final position and bounces back. Follow through creates the feeling of physical reality ‚Äî that the object has mass, momentum, and imperfection. Without anticipation and follow through, motion feels dead.

B

Motion Language

Motion isn't decoration. It's a language with grammar, vocabulary, and meaning.

Kinetic Typography

Text that moves with meaning. Kinetic typography treats words as visual and temporal objects ‚Äî their movement, scale, speed, and rhythm all add layers of meaning to the words themselves. The word "CRASH" slamming into frame is different from "crash" floating gently. Kinetic typography transforms reading into experiencing. The principles: the movement should reflect the word's meaning (semantic motion), the rhythm should match the intended reading pace, and the hierarchy should guide the eye through the message. Bad kinetic typography: everything moves all the time. Good kinetic typography: motion punctuates meaning.

Visual Rhythm

Just like music has beats and rests, motion design has visual rhythm ‚Äî the pattern of action and stillness over time. A sequence of elements appearing at regular intervals creates a beat. Breaking that pattern creates emphasis. Three elements enter quickly, the fourth enters slowly ‚Äî the fourth becomes the star. Visual rhythm creates anticipation: once the audience learns the pattern, they lean in when it breaks. The best motion graphics feel like compositions ‚Äî they have a tempo, bridges, verses, and a crescendo.

Motion Hierarchy

In static design, hierarchy is achieved through size, color, and position. In motion design, hierarchy adds a temporal dimension: what moves first is what matters most. The primary element animates in with the most elaborate motion. Secondary elements follow. Background elements move subtly or remain static. If everything moves simultaneously with equal energy, the viewer's eye doesn't know where to look ‚Äî you've created visual noise. Motion hierarchy = temporal priority + movement emphasis.

Motion Continuity

Motion continuity means that movements across scenes or compositions feel connected ‚Äî objects leave one scene in a direction and enter the next from the corresponding side. Speed feels consistent. The visual language established in the first shot persists. Without motion continuity, sequences feel disjointed and amateurish. It's the motion graphics equivalent of cinematic continuity editing ‚Äî the audience should never feel a jarring disconnect between moments.

Meaningful Transitions

A transition is not a decoration between scenes ‚Äî it's a statement about the relationship between them. A dissolve implies connection or transformation. A hard cut implies contrast or collision. A morph transition says "these two things are one." A wipe says "time is passing." Every transition carries narrative information. If a transition exists only because "it looked cool," it's noise. The question: "What does this transition mean?"

Motion as Emotion

Different types of motion trigger different emotional responses:
Smooth, slow, flowing = calm, elegant, peaceful
Quick, sharp, snappy = energetic, playful, urgent
Jittery, erratic = anxiety, instability, danger
Elastic, bouncy = fun, youthful, approachable
Heavy, labored = serious, weighty, important
Floating, drifting = dreamlike, detached, ethereal
Movement is inherently emotional because our bodies understand physics instinctively. We feel the weight, resistance, and effort of animated objects. This is why easing matters so much ‚Äî the physics of motion triggers empathic response.

Calm vs Aggressive Motion

Calm motion: Long easing curves, gentle speed changes, wide arcs, soft landing, gradual transitions, low contrast between speeds. Creates a sense of professionalism, luxury, or contemplation. Think Apple product videos ‚Äî everything moves slowly, intentionally, with precision.
Aggressive motion: Snap easing (quick acceleration, instant stop), linear segments, overshoot, screen shake, rapid scale changes, hard cuts. Creates energy, impact, urgency. Think sports graphics, action movie titles, and tech product launches.
Mixing the two within a single piece creates contrast and drama ‚Äî a long calm build followed by an aggressive burst is exponentially more impactful than aggression alone.

C

Technical Core (Software-Agnostic)

These concepts apply everywhere ‚Äî After Effects, Blender, CSS, Remotion, AI generators. The principles don't change with the tool.

Keyframing Basics

A keyframe marks a specific property value at a specific point in time. The software interpolates between keyframes to create motion. Keyframe at frame 0: object at position X=0. Keyframe at frame 30: object at position X=500. The software generates all 30 intermediate positions. The magic is in what happens between the keyframes ‚Äî and that's controlled by easing. The minimum keyframes principle: use the fewest keyframes possible to describe the motion. Fewer keyframes = cleaner, more controllable animation.

Graph Editor Explained

The graph editor visualizes how a property changes over time as a curve. The X-axis is time. The Y-axis is the property value. A straight line = constant speed. A curved line = acceleration or deceleration. A steep curve = fast change. A flat curve = no change. This is the most powerful animation tool and the one most beginners avoid. Working in the graph editor gives you pixel-perfect control over feeling. Two animations can have identical start and end points but feel completely different based on their graph curves.

B√©zier Curves Animation

B√©zier curves use control handles to create smooth, adjustable curves between keyframes. In animation, they define easing behavior. Pull a handle up and right = slow start, fast end. Pull a handle down and left = fast start, slow end. The handles give you precise control over the character of motion ‚Äî the difference between a gentle settle and an aggressive snap. In CSS: cubic-bezier(0.42, 0, 0.58, 1). In After Effects: the graph editor handles. The concept is identical everywhere.

Alpha Channels

An alpha channel is the transparency layer of an image or video. RGBA: Red, Green, Blue, and Alpha. Alpha = 0 means fully transparent. Alpha = 255 means fully opaque. Alpha channels allow you to composite elements on top of each other ‚Äî text over video, a character over a background, effects over footage. Without understanding alpha, compositing is impossible. Key insight: when rendering motion graphics elements for compositing, always render with an alpha channel (ProRes 4444, PNG sequence, or WebM VP9).

Compositing Basics

Compositing is the art of layering multiple visual elements into a single final image. Foreground over background. Text over footage. VFX elements over live action. Compositing is not just stacking ‚Äî it's matching. The composited elements must match in lighting, color, noise, motion blur, and perspective to feel like they belong together. Bad compositing: elements look "pasted on." Good compositing: you can't tell what's real and what isn't. Skills needed: matte extraction, color matching, edge refinement, and layer order.

Blend Modes Explained

Blend modes determine how two layers interact mathematically:
Normal ‚Äî Top layer covers bottom. Standard stacking.
Multiply ‚Äî Darkens. White disappears, black stays. Great for shadows and overlaying textures onto footage.
Screen ‚Äî Lightens. Black disappears, white stays. Perfect for fire, light leaks, and glows over dark footage.
Overlay ‚Äî Combines Multiply and Screen. Increases contrast. Used for texture integration and color grading effects.
Add ‚Äî Adds brightness values. Creates extreme glows and light effects.
Understanding blend modes means understanding how color values interact mathematically. This knowledge transfers across every tool: Photoshop, After Effects, Blender, CSS (mix-blend-mode), and even AI compositing workflows.

Masking Animation

A mask defines which part of a layer is visible. Animating the mask ‚Äî its shape, position, or feather ‚Äî over time creates reveals, wipes, and selective visibility. A text element revealed by a traveling mask feels deliberate and polished. A circular mask expanding from center creates a spotlight reveal. Masks can track motion, follow shapes, and create complex animated mattes. Masking is the bridge between static design and temporal design ‚Äî it answers the question: "What does the viewer see now, and what is hidden for later?"

Parallax Motion

Parallax is the principle that objects at different distances move at different speeds relative to the viewer. Close objects move fast, distant objects move slow. This creates the illusion of 3D depth in a 2D composition. In motion design: separating a scene into foreground, midground, and background layers and moving them at different speeds during a camera pan creates rich, dimensional space. Used everywhere ‚Äî title sequences, social media posts, explainer videos, and web design scroll effects. The depth is an illusion, but the brain reads it as real.
