I. Scene Archetypes

10 universal scene types. Each with a core emotional shift and a complete spatial/light/lens/movement/editing grammar.

Archetype 01

Confrontation Scene

Power vs Power / Truth vs Defense
"Control ‚Üí Loss of Control"
Space
Start in balanced two-shot. Gradually break into singles. Increase physical distance OR push into aggressive close-ups.
Light
Start even. Introduce shadow separation as conflict escalates.
Lens
35‚Äì50mm for neutrality. Shift to telephoto as tension peaks (compression = suffocation).
Movement
Start locked off. Introduce slow push-ins from both sides. One character ends closer to lens (visual dominance).
Editing
Even back-and-forth. Interrupt pattern when truth drops.
Structural Trick: Cut to reaction before accusation lands. The face receiving truth is more powerful than the face delivering it.
Archetype 02

Reunion Scene

Separation ‚Üí Emotional Release
"Distance ‚Üí Collapse of Distance"
Space
Begin with obstructed view. Reveal full frame when recognition hits.
Light
Naturalistic. Increase backlight subtly during embrace.
Lens
Medium focal length. Shallow depth when physical contact happens.
Movement
Slow push or orbit. Slight handheld warmth.
Editing
Hold longer than audience expects. Avoid overcutting.
Structural Trick: Delay eye contact. Let the audience feel the anticipation of connection before it arrives.
Archetype 03

Death Scene

Presence ‚Üí Irreversible Absence
"Life ‚Üí Void"
Space
Increase negative space immediately after death. Show environment continuing unchanged.
Light
Do not dramatize. Let light remain indifferent.
Lens
Wider lens for realism. Or static medium for detachment.
Movement
Stop movement after moment. Freeze visual energy.
Editing
Remove music. Hold.
Structural Trick: Cut to ordinary object instead of body. The mundane continuing while life has stopped is devastating. See Presence vs Absence.
Archetype 04

Revelation Scene

Ignorance ‚Üí New Knowledge
"Confusion ‚Üí Clarity (or collapse)"
Space
Character framed off-center. Re-center once realization hits.
Light
Shift contrast. Introduce edge light subtly.
Lens
Push from medium to close. Increase isolation.
Movement
Slow controlled push. Or sudden stillness.
Editing
Cut to memory flashes (if internal). Or remove all cuts and hold.
Structural Trick: Sound drops before realization registers. The silence primes the audience to receive impact.
Archetype 05

Betrayal Scene

Trust ‚Üí Fracture
"Shared Frame ‚Üí Separate Frames"
Space
Start in shared two-shot. End in isolated singles.
Light
Introduce shadow barrier (doorframe, pillar).
Lens
Telephoto for emotional compression.
Movement
Very slow push on betrayed face. Betrayer remains static.
Editing
Delay betrayed reaction. Cut wide after impact (emotional drop).
Structural Trick: Let betrayal land in silence. No score, no sound design ‚Äî just the face and the void. See Emotion #15: Betrayal.
Archetype 06

Chase Scene

Safety ‚Üí Survival
"Stability ‚Üí Instability"
Space
Narrow corridors. Diagonal compositions.
Light
High contrast. Moving shadows.
Lens
Wide lens close to subject. Deep focus for spatial awareness.
Movement
Aggressive handheld. Rapid forward motion.
Editing
Acceleration. Shorter and shorter shot duration.
Structural Trick: Sudden still frame before final burst. The pause amplifies the explosion that follows.
Archetype 07

Intimate Confession

Internal Burden ‚Üí Vulnerability
"Guarded ‚Üí Exposed"
Space
Close proximity. Minimal background distraction.
Light
Soft directional. Eye light crucial.
Lens
50‚Äì85mm shallow focus.
Movement
Static. Micro push only.
Editing
Hold on listener more than speaker.
Structural Trick: Cut away before response is spoken. Let the audience imagine the answer ‚Äî what they imagine is always more powerful than what you show.
Archetype 08

Triumph Scene

Doubt ‚Üí Victory
"Constraint ‚Üí Expansion"
Space
Begin compressed frame. End in wide open vertical composition.
Light
Backlight flare. Increase contrast.
Lens
Start tighter. End wider.
Movement
Rising crane or tilt up.
Editing
Slow down instead of speed up.
Structural Trick: Silence before celebration. The gap between victory and reaction is where the audience feels the weight. See Emotion #23: Triumph.
Archetype 09

Isolation Scene

Belonging ‚Üí Disconnection
"Integrated ‚Üí Separate"
Space
Character moves opposite crowd direction. Wide shot with subject small.
Light
Cooler tones. Environmental practicals.
Lens
Deep focus wide.
Movement
Lateral tracking.
Editing
Let ambient sound overpower dialogue.
Structural Trick: The world doesn't pause for isolation. Keep environmental activity at full volume ‚Äî the character's disconnection is amplified by the world's indifference. See Emotion #19: Alienation.
Archetype 10

Transformation Scene

Old Identity ‚Üí New Identity
"Internal shift becomes external behavior."
Space
Start cluttered. End clean and centered.
Light
Start flat. Introduce directional sculpting.
Lens
Push progressively closer.
Movement
Camera becomes more stable over time.
Editing
Reduce cuts as confidence increases.
Structural Trick: Mirror shot at beginning and end. Same framing, same angle ‚Äî but everything has changed. The audience sees the transformation through identical composition with a different subject inside it.

IV. Anti-Clich√© System

The audience expects: Sad = blue, Love = sunset, Anger = red, Fear = darkness. Do not obey emotional color stereotypes.

1. Counter-Expectation Principle

If audience expects dark, try bright indifference. The disconnect between visual tone and emotional content amplifies the feeling ‚Äî the world's refusal to match the emotion makes it worse.

Example: Death in harsh midday sun.

2. Remove Obvious Symbol

No rain for sadness. No mirror for identity crisis (unless subverted). If using a symbolic object, introduce it before the emotion appears ‚Äî plant the symbol in a neutral context so it accumulates meaning rather than announcing it.

See Symbolism vs Obvious Imagery.

3. Environmental Indifference

The most powerful emotion arrives when the world does not react. Character collapses emotionally ‚Äî traffic continues normally. The indifference of the environment amplifies the isolation of the feeling.

Example: Breakdown in a busy supermarket. Sound of checkout beeps continues.

4. Emotional Contrast Injection

Place calm framing during rage. Place static wide during breakdown. The contrast between visual composure and emotional chaos increases realism ‚Äî real life doesn't have a cinematographer matching mood to lighting on demand.

5. Withhold Visual Emphasis

If the moment is huge ‚Äî do less. Underplay visually. Let performance breathe. The more important the moment, the more the camera should trust the actor and the audience. Over-styling a climax betrays insecurity in the material.
